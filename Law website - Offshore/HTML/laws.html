Substantive Criminal Law

Criminal law is a branch of law that deals with behaviors that are classified as offenses against the state,
society, x or the public, even though the immediate victim may be an individual. Criminal law sets out the
rules and guidelines for the prosecution, defense, and punishment of people who commit crimes.It is
divided into two broad categories:

This defines specific crimes, penalties, and defenses. Crimes are generally divided into: Felonies: Serious
crimes like murder, rape, and armed robbery, usually punishable by more than a year in prison.
Misdemeanors: Lesser crimes like petty theft or vandalism, typically punishable by less than a year in jail
or fines.

Substantive criminal law also outlines the elements of a crime that must be proven to secure a conviction,
such as: Actus Reus (the criminal act) Mens Rea (the criminal intent)

Common defenses include: Insanity: The defendant was not mentally capable of understanding their actions.
Self-Defense: The
defendant committed the act to protect themselves or others from harm. Duress: The defendant was forced
to commit the crime under threat of harm. Entrapment: The defendant was induced by law enforcement
to commit the crime.
Criminal law varies by jurisdiction, with different states or countries having their own criminal codes, but
many principles of criminal law are common across legal systems worldwide.

Substantive Family Law

Family law is a legal practice area that deals with matters related to family relationships. It encompasses a
wide range of topics and issues that impact individuals in personal and familial settings. Some of the key
areas covered by family law include:

Marriage and Divorce Marriage Laws: These laws govern the legal requirements for getting married, including
age, consent, and formalities like licensing. Divorce: This is one of the most common aspects of family law,
dealing with the dissolution of marriage. Divorce law includes: Division of property and assets Child custody
and visitation rights Alimony (spousal support) Child support .

Family law courts decide who will have legal and physical custody of children after a separation or divorce.
These decisions are based on the best interest of the child, considering factors like parental fitness, the child’s
relationship with each parent, and the child’s well-being. Visitation rights are awarded to the non-custodial parent,
allowing regular interaction and bonding with the child.

Family law addresses domestic violence cases, providing legal remedies to protect victims. These remedies include
restraining orders, which legally prohibit an abuser from contacting or approaching the victim .

Housing Law

Housing law is a branch of law that deals with the rights and responsibilities of landlords and tenants.
It encompasses various aspects, including leasing agreements, tenant rights, eviction processes, and
housing discrimination. Here are some key components of housing law:

Lease agreements outline the terms under which a tenant can occupy a rental property. This includes rent
amount, duration of tenancy, and specific responsibilities for both the landlord and tenant.

Tenants have rights that protect them from unfair practices, such as: Right to Habitability:
The property must be safe and livable. Privacy Rights: Landlords typically
cannot enter the rental property without notice, except in emergencies. Protection Against Retaliation:
Tenants cannot be evicted or harassed for exercising their rights.

Housing law also prohibits discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, or disability.
Laws such as the Fair Housing Act in the U.S. ensure that everyone has equal access to housing opportunities.
